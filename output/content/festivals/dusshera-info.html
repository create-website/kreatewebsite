What is Dussehra?

Dussehra, also known as Vijayadashami, is one of India's most significant festivals, celebrating the ultimate victory of good over evil. It's a day filled with deep symbolism, ancient stories, and vibrant community celebrations. This section provides a snapshot of the core principles of the festival.

When is it Celebrated?

Two Epic Victories

The day celebrates two major triumphs: Lord Rama's defeat of the ten-headed demon king Ravana, and Goddess Durga's victory over the buffalo demon Mahishasur.

The Epic Legends

The foundations of Dussehra are built on two of Hinduism's most powerful stories. Below, we explore the climax of the Ramayana and the valor of Goddess Durga, both tales highlighting the inevitable downfall of evil in the face of divine power and righteousness.

Rama's Victory Over Ravana

The most widely recognized story is from the epic Ramayana. Dussehra commemorates the day Lord Rama, the seventh avatar of Vishnu, defeated the ten-headed demon king of Lanka, Ravana, who had abducted Rama's wife, Sita. After a monumental battle, Rama's victory symbolized the conquest of ego, injustice, and cruelty.

Durga's Triumph Over Mahishasur

In many parts of India, particularly in the east and south, Vijayadashami celebrates Goddess Durga's victory over the shape-shifting buffalo demon, Mahishasur. Endowed with a boon of near-invincibility, he created chaos across the universe. The gods combined their energies to create Durga, who, after a nine-night battle (Navaratri), finally slayed him on the tenth day. This victory celebrates the power of Shakti (divine feminine energy).

The Ten-Day Journey to Victory

Dussehra is the culmination of a ten-day period of worship and fasting. The first nine nights, known as Navaratri, are dedicated to celebrating the nine forms of Goddess Durga. This timeline highlights the progression, leading to the final day of triumph.

Days 1-3: The Awakening

The first three days are dedicated to Durga in her fierce forms (Kali, Lakshmi, Saraswati) to destroy impurities and vices. It begins with the Ghatasthapana ritual.

Days 4-6: The Abundance

The focus shifts to worshipping the Goddess as Lakshmi, the bestower of spiritual wealth and prosperity, bringing a more gentle and abundant quality to the festivities.

Days 7-9: The Wisdom

The final days of Navaratri are dedicated to Goddess Saraswati, the embodiment of knowledge and art. This period culminates in Maha Navami, the ninth day, with special pujas and rituals.

Day 10: VIJAYADASHAMI

The grand finale. It is the day of victory, marking the triumph of good over evil. This is when the effigies are burnt, processions take place, and the festival reaches its joyous peak.
