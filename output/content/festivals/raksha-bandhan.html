Raksha Bandhan

"The Bond of Protection"

This interactive experience explores Raksha Bandhan, a festival that venerates the relationship between brothers and sisters, but whose meaning has expanded to embrace a wide spectrum of chosen family and protective bonds. Journey through its rich tapestry of mythology, contested history, and modern global adaptations to understand how a simple thread became a profound symbol of love and obligation.

A Festival in Flux

Raksha Bandhan is not a static tradition. It varies significantly across regions and has adapted globally. Explore its diverse expressions and see how the festival is being reinterpreted in the 21st century.

Regional Diversity

In West Bengal, it's Jhulan Purnima, celebrating Radha-Krishna. In Maharashtra, it's Narali Poornima, where fishermen offer coconuts to the sea. In Rajasthan, a 'Lumba Rakhi' is tied to the sister-in-law, extending the bond.

Global Diaspora

Across the world, the diaspora maintains tradition through community events. In the UK and Australia, the ritual has been adapted, with rakhis tied on police and soldiers as a gesture of gratitude and respect for community protectors.

Modern Interpretations

Today, the festival embraces inclusivity. Sisters tie rakhis on each other, friends exchange them, and eco-friendly rakhis made of seeds or jute are gaining popularity, reflecting a shift towards mutual support and sustainability.
