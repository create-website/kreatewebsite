What is Eid?

"Eid" in Arabic means "feast" or "festival." In Islam, there are two major Eid festivals celebrated by millions of Muslims worldwide. Both are joyous occasions for prayer, feasting, charity, and spending time with loved ones, but they commemorate different spiritual events.

The Two Eids of the Year

The two major festivals, Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha, occur at different times of the year and commemorate different significant events in Islamic history.

Eid al-Fitr: The Festival of Breaking the Fast

Celebrated on the first day of Shawwal, the month after Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr marks the joyous end of the month-long fast. It is a reward for the devotion and self-control practiced during Ramadan. Before the Eid prayer, Muslims are required to give a special charity, *Zakat al-Fitr*, to the poor so they too can celebrate. The day is filled with feasting, with a particular emphasis on sweets, earning it the nickname "Sweet Eid."

Eid al-Adha: The Festival of Sacrifice

This Eid, also known as the "Greater Eid," is celebrated during the month of Dhul Hijjah, at the conclusion of the annual Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca. It commemorates the willingness of the Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) to sacrifice his son as an act of obedience to God. Just as he was about to do so, God provided a ram to sacrifice instead. To honor this, Muslims who can afford it sacrifice an animal (like a sheep or goat) and divide the meat into three parts: one for the family, one for relatives and friends, and one for the poor.
