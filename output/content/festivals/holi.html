What is Holi?

Holi is one of India's most vibrant and exuberant festivals, welcoming the arrival of spring and celebrating the triumph of good over evil. It's a day of joyous abandon, where people chase each other with colored powders and water, temporarily erasing social boundaries in a flurry of fun.

The Legends of Color

Holi is steeped in rich mythology that gives depth to its playful exterior. The festival's two primary legends speak of unwavering devotion and divine, playful love, forming the spiritual backbone of the celebration.

The Legend of Holika & Prahlad

The main legend celebrates the victory of Prince Prahlad, a devout follower of Lord Vishnu, over his arrogant father, King Hiranyakashipu. The king wanted everyone to worship him, but Prahlad refused. The king's evil sister, Holika, who was immune to fire, tricked Prahlad into sitting on a pyre with her. However, due to his unwavering devotion, Prahlad was unharmed while Holika burned. The bonfire on the eve of Holi symbolizes this victory of good over evil.

The Love of Radha & Krishna

In the Braj region of India, Holi is celebrated as a festival of divine love, commemorating the playful romance of Radha and Krishna. Legend says that the young, dark-skinned Krishna was envious of Radha's fair complexion. His mother Yashoda playfully suggested he apply color to Radha's face. Krishna did so, and their playful coloring of each other's faces became a tradition, celebrated today as the joyous and romantic aspect of Holi.

The Two Days of Holi

Holi is primarily a two-day festival, each with its distinct character and set of rituals, transitioning from solemn purification to uninhibited joy.

Day 1: Holika Dahan (Choti Holi)

On the eve of Holi, communities gather to light large bonfires. This ritual, known as Holika Dahan, symbolizes the burning of evil spirits and the triumph of good. People sing and dance around the fire, celebrating the victory of faith.
