The Meaning of the Mountain

Worshiping the Protector

**Govardhan Puja**, also known as **Annakut** (meaning "a mountain of food"), is celebrated on the first lunar day of the bright fortnight of the Kartika month, immediately following Diwali. It commemorates the day **Lord Krishna** defeated the ego of the Vedic deity Indra by successfully lifting the **Govardhan Hill** on his little finger for seven days to protect the cowherders and villagers of Vrindavan from torrential rains. The festival is a reminder of the importance of devotion over ritualism and the vital connection between humanity and nature, which Govardhan Hill represents as a source of sustenance.

The Legend of Lord Krishna and Indra

Krishna Lifts the Mountain

Centuries ago, the villagers of Vrindavan prepared an elaborate offering to Lord Indra, the deity of rain. Lord Krishna, still a young boy, convinced them to worship the **Govardhan Hill** instead, arguing that the hill and the cows were their true source of life and sustenance. Enraged by this slight, Indra unleashed a massive storm, flooding Vrindavan. To save the people, Krishna miraculously lifted the entire Govardhan Hill on his left pinky finger and held it up for seven days, sheltering all the inhabitants and animals beneath it. Realizing his arrogance, Indra apologized, establishing **Govardhan Puja** as a tribute to Krishna's compassion and the sanctity of the natural world.

The Steps of Govardhan Worship

The rituals revolve around creating a symbolic Govardhan Hill and offering the Annakut feast. Follow these steps to understand how devotees celebrate this day.

1. Hill Creation

A symbolic Govardhan Hill is crafted, usually using **cow dung (Gobar)**, symbolizing the connection to nature and rural life.

2. Decorative Elements

The mound is decorated with flowers, grass, and clay figures of cows, Lord Krishna, and the villagers to recreate the scene of protection.

3. Annakut Preparation

Devotees prepare a **mountain of vegetarian food (Annakut)**, often consisting of 56 or 108 different dishes (Chhappan Bhog) as an offering.

4. Circumambulation (Parikrama)

Ritual worship (Puja) is performed, and devotees walk around the symbolic hill, praying for Krishna's blessings and protection.
